On January 28, 2026, the first obligations of the EU AI Act came into force, marking the beginning of the world's most comprehensive regulatory regime for artificial intelligence. Contrary to what many expected — that the rules would be lenient or delayed — the European Commission was strict: AI systems classified as "high-risk" now require conformity assessment before being placed on the market.

The AI Act classifies AI systems into four risk categories: minimal, limited, high, and unacceptable. The obligations that took effect in January 2026 focus on:

- **High-risk systems**: recruitment, credit, insurance, justice, migration, and critical infrastructure. They require conformity assessment, technical documentation, data governance, and mandatory human oversight.
- **General-purpose AI (GPAI) systems**: models like GPT-4, Claude 4, and Llama 4 must publish detailed summaries of training data and implement copyright policies.
- **Direct prohibitions**: social scoring systems, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, and behavioral manipulation are banned.

## The Impact on Technology Companies

Compliance costs are not trivial. Companies operating in Europe estimate spending between US$500,000 and US$5 million per system classified as high-risk, depending on complexity. For startups, this cost can be prohibitive — and the AI Act is already generating what experts call an "AI divide": companies with resources for compliance and companies that simply cannot operate in Europe.

Meta, for example, announced on January 30 that it will not launch Llama 4 in Europe until GPAI rules are clearer. OpenAI, on the other hand, already has an 80-person team dedicated to the AI Act and states that GPT-4.5 will be compliant from launch.

## Effects on the Brazilian Market

Brazilian companies that operate in Europe or use AI systems developed by European suppliers are also affected. The AI Act has extraterritorial reach: any company that places an AI system on the EU market or whose outputs are consumed by users in the EU is subject to the rules.

For the Brazilian ecosystem, the AI Act functions as a de facto standard. Large local companies — banks, insurers, retailers — are already voluntarily adopting AI Act standards as a benchmark for best practices, anticipating possible Brazilian regulation. Bill PL 2338/2023 (the Brazilian AI Legal Framework) is stalled in Congress but tends to mirror the European model when resumed.

## What This Means

The EU AI Act is no longer a future threat — it is a reality with concrete fines and obligations. Companies that have not yet started mapping their AI systems by risk category are behind. For the technology market as a whole, European regulation is setting the global compliance standard, and ignoring it means being locked out of the world's second-largest technology market. The message for Brazilian CTOs and CISOs: start the mapping today, before enforcement reaches you.
