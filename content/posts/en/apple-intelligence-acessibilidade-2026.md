On April 28, 2026, Apple presented a set of AI-powered accessibility features that, for at least one day, shifted the focus away from the model arms race and placed technology at the service of those who need it most. In a market obsessed with parameters and benchmarks, Apple reminded us that AI can — and should — have a profound social impact.

## Computer Vision for Navigation

The new Door Detection 2.0 feature, now powered by a semantic segmentation model running on the M5 chip's Neural Engine, enables visually impaired people to navigate complex indoor environments with centimeter-level precision. The model identifies doors, hallways, stairs, elevators, and obstacles, and describes the environment in spatial audio with directional guidance: "door to your left, 3 meters, opens inward."

The novelty in 2026 is the ability to memorize floor plans. The iPhone creates a 3D map of the environment after being walked through once, and on subsequent visits it automatically recognizes the location and provides point-to-point navigation. It works in malls, hospitals, airports, and offices without requiring additional infrastructure — just the iPhone's camera.

## Contextual Voice Assistant

Siri received a significant upgrade with the Apple Context Engine, a 7B parameter language model running entirely on-device. Unlike assistants that process isolated commands, Siri now maintains conversation context, anaphoric references, and memory of user preferences — without sending data to the cloud.

For accessibility, the impact is enormous. Users with reduced mobility can now issue compound commands: "remember the email that came yesterday from the doctor? remind me to reply tomorrow at 10am and open the attached exams." Siri understands, executes, and confirms each step. On-device processing latency dropped to 300ms per inference thanks to the M5's 24-core Neural Engine.

## Real-Time Sign Language Translation

The most impactful feature is Live Translation for Brazilian Sign Language (Libras) and other sign languages. Using the TrueDepth camera, the iPhone captures hand movements, facial expressions, and body language in real time, translates to text or audio, and vice versa — generating an animated avatar that signs the response.

The model is a 3D transformer trained on 50 thousand hours of sign language conversations (Libras, ASL, LSE, LSF), with manual annotation of 200 thousand distinct gestures. Accuracy in controlled environments is 96% for Libras, 93% for ASL. In noisy environments (low lighting, complex background), accuracy drops to 82% — but is sufficient for functional communication.

## Privacy as Competitive Advantage

All accessibility features run entirely on-device. No image, audio, or conversation data leaves the user's hardware. Apple is using this as a direct competitive advantage against Google and Microsoft, whose similar features rely on cloud processing. For accessibility, where user trust is critical — especially for people with conditions protected by medical privacy laws — Apple's on-device features are a real differentiator.

## What This Means

Apple Intelligence shows that the AI race does not have to be only about larger and more expensive models. The on-device approach, focused on accessibility and privacy, is consistent with Apple's historical strategy — and may be the most sustainable long-term. The message to the market is clear: competitors that neglect accessibility and privacy are losing not only a significant market segment (1.3 billion people with disabilities worldwide) but also the trust of users who value control over their data. AI that transforms lives does not have to be in the cloud — sometimes, the best place for it is in the user's pocket.
